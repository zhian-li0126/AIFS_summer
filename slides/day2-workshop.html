Hands-On Exploration Ideas

  1. Use one sensor to collect data every few seconds.
  2. Change the environment and watch how the readings respond.
  3. Plot or record the data over time.
  4. Decide what action the system should take when the value is too high or too low.
Example: Put your hand near a temperature/humidity sensor. Does the reading change? How quickly?

Before You Touch the Hardware

Safety:

  • Keep water away from laptops and power strips.
  • Do not force wires into pins.
  • Ask before moving power connections.

Good engineering habit:

  • Check wiring first.
  • Run the simplest code first.
  • Change one thing at a time.

DHT11 Station: Temperature + Humidity

The DHT11 is like a small weather station.

It reports two values: air temperature and relative humidity.

Try it: breathe gently near the sensor or place your hand near it.

Watch: Which value changes first: temperature or humidity?

PAR Sensor Example: Is Every Plant Getting Enough Light?

Question: Are plants near the edge receiving less light than plants in the center?

Data: PAR sensor readings at different locations.

Action: adjust LED position, height, or brightness.

Classroom challenge:

  1. Measure light at three places.
  2. Compare the numbers.
  3. Find the “bright spot” and “dark spot.”

pH Station: Is the Water Too Acidic or Basic?

pH tells us whether water is acidic, neutral, or basic.

For plants, pH is like a nutrient gatekeeper. If pH is too high or too low, some nutrients become harder for roots to absorb.

Try it: compare two safe sample solutions provided by the instructor.

Record: Which sample has the lower pH?

EC Station: How Much Fertilizer Is in the Water?

EC means electrical conductivity. It estimates how many dissolved ions are in the water.

Metaphor: EC is like a saltiness meter for nutrient solution. It does not tell us each nutrient separately, but it tells us the total amount of dissolved fertilizer.

Try it: compare clean water and nutrient solution.

Question: Which one should have higher EC?

Ultrasonic Sensor Basics

Idea: The sensor sends out sound, waits for the echo, and uses the return time to estimate distance.

From Reading to Decision Rule

A sensor reading becomes useful when we connect it to a decision.

DHT11

If temperature is high, turn on a fan.

PAR

If light is low, turn on or raise the LED brightness.

Ultrasonic

If water level is low, send an alert or start refill.

Plot the Story

When values change over time, a graph can show the story faster than a table.

Look for patterns: rising, falling, stable, noisy, or sudden jumps.

Example pattern

Humidity rises when your hand is near the sensor, then slowly returns to normal.

Workshop Takeaways

  • Sensors help computers understand the physical world.
  • Greenhouse data becomes useful when we connect it to plant needs.
  • Arduino and Raspberry Pi can collect, display, and respond to sensor readings.
  • Good engineering starts with careful observation and simple experiments.
