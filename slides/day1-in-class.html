What is Controlled Environment Agriculture?

Controlled Environment Agriculture (CEA) uses engineering systems to control the plant environment.

Instead of letting weather fully decide plant growth, CEA adjusts conditions such as light, temperature, humidity, water, nutrients, and air flow.

Simple example: If lettuce grows slowly because light is too low, a CEA system can increase LED intensity or extend photoperiod.

Why Control the Environment?

Consistency

Reduce variation caused by weather.

Efficiency

Use water, nutrients, space, and energy more carefully.

Monitoring

Collect data and respond quickly to plant stress.

Hydroponics: Growing Without Soil

Hydroponics grows plants using water and dissolved nutrients instead of soil.

The root zone becomes an engineered system: we control water flow, oxygen, nutrient concentration, pH, and temperature.

Example: Lettuce roots sit in or near a nutrient solution. The plant absorbs nitrate, potassium, calcium, magnesium, phosphate, sulfate, and water through the roots.

Three Hydroponic Systems

NFT

Nutrient Film Technique

Thin flowing nutrient solution passes over roots.

DWC

Deep Water Culture

Roots are suspended in aerated nutrient solution.

Flood-and-Drain

Root zone is periodically flooded, then drained.

CEA as a Feedback System

Sensor reads environment
→
Controller compares to target
→
Actuator changes system
→
Plant response

Error = Target − Measured Value

If error is large, the control system needs to respond.

Example: Temperature Control

Target: 24°C

Measured: 29°C

Error: 24 − 29 = −5°C

Possible action: increase ventilation, shading, or cooling.

Day 1 Key Takeaways

  • CEA uses engineering to control plant environments.
  • Hydroponics replaces soil with water, nutrients, oxygen, and support structures.
  • NFT, DWC, and flood-and-drain systems manage the root zone differently.
  • Data, sensors, and control are central to modern indoor farming.
